The Case Particle I

37: The Case Particle  I: The Attribute Marker

The case particle is essential to marking phrases as attributes. Although it possesses various functions, we will be focusing only on how it is used as the attribute marker. 

The Case Particle : The Japanese "Of"

The most essential role that the case particle has is establishing Noun A as an attribute of Noun B. 


Terminology Note: The case particle is also known in Japanese grammar as a (attributive particle). Noun A (the modifier noun) = , and Noun B (the modified noun) = .

Grammar Pattern: Noun A + + Noun B
 i.   page(s) of a book    or    book('s) page(s)
ii.          shape of a cloud      or    cloud('s) shape
iii.         exterior of a house    or   house('s) exterior

The attribute marking function of  is often translated as "of" or even as "'s" in English, but how do these grammatically different translations relate to ?


The definition of "of" in English is defined as "expressing the relationship between a part and a whole." Because "of" is a preposition, it is actually marking the "whole" as the attribute of the part. It's just that the word order is the opposite than 's, which has the same function. 


The particle doesn't follow a different logic, but Japanese word order prevents the flipping of the modifier noun and the modified noun. In Japanese, Noun A is always the modifier, and it is 's job as the attribute marker to label it as the bigger picture. Since its word order is fixed, it may be easier to view it as the Japanese 's instead.


What happens, then, in Japanese when the "bigger picture" (modifier noun) and the "smaller picture" (modified noun) are flipped? The pictures swap roles entirely.

iv.     House outside (a certain bounds)

In iv., the "outside" is a place far removed and is far larger in scale than talking about the exterior "part" of a house. We can also see that what the sentence is about also shifts focus. The subject is always Noun B, not Noun A. 


The bigger picture adds context to what the speaker wants to talk about. As one can imagine, then, the sort of contexts that can be viewed as attributes can all be categorized to better help understand how functions.


Let's take a look at one more example. The difference between v. and vii. is a complete 180 as the relationships being described are from opposite standpoints. As for the particle itself, its role does not change. 


v.
The secretary's boss


Who is the boss affiliated with in the big picture? The secretary. 

vi.
The boss' secretary.  


Who is the secretary affiliated with in the big picture? The boss.


There are occasionally situations in which flipping Noun A and Noun B doesn't result in much of a change of meaning. This occurs when one of them is a verbal noun. 


vii.
Expert in hunting

viii.

Expert hunting


It must be noted that there is no limit to how many attributes can be linked together with . Meaning, Noun A may be the attribute of Noun B, Noun B may be the attribute of Noun C, etc. The longer the chain, the narrower the following noun will be, capturing more "smaller pictures."

Since we know Noun A is the "big picture" modifier, it would make sense that serves to show where Noun B belongs. In turn, Noun A may be the owner of possession, the "whole" of a part, affiliation (often a place), as well as the place where Noun B belongs.

The Omission of

There are several situations in which the case particle may be omitted. In any case, its function ought to be understood whether it is present or not. 


In native vocabulary, the omission of is permissible so long as Noun A wouldn't otherwise function as an object. With two or more Sino-Japanese words, the longer the chain, the more likely is dropped, but omission is not likely when words of mixed etymology are put together.

As alluded to earlier, there is natural overlap between the particle and other grammatical items which share the ability of marking an attribute.
