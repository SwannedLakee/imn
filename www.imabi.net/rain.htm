Rain

341: Rain

Rain, or as it is called in Japanese, /ame/ is formed from liquid droplets of water that have condensed from atmospheric water vapor that then fall from the sky under gravity. It has been the source of worship and reverence from cultures across the world due to both its nurturing and destructive powers. 


Wherever you are in the world, the air contains some amount of water vapor. This water vapor may originate from evaporation () off the oceans (), lakes (), or the ground () or it may be provided by transpiration () from plants (). Rain falls from the sky out of clouds (), but rain is also not the only form of precipitation. Furthermore, the process of water going from liquid to gas to liquid/solid and so on creates a cycle known as the "water cycle." 


Though words associated with rain such as (rain), (raincloud), (thunderstorm), etc. may seem simple, the Japanese language is rich with rain-related words. Just the words to describe the various of aspects of precipitation thus far requires an extensive knowledge of weather vocabulary, much of which you would naturally learn from watching weather reports ().  


On other planets, what falls as "rain" may not be made of water, but in this discussion, we'll mostly be talking about the H2O that falls out of our sky.

The Water Cycle on Earth

On Earth (), the water cycle ()is driven by solar energy () in a continuous cycle of water changing between its solid phase (), liquid phase (), and gas phase ().  The general flow of the water cycle is evaporation/transpiration ()  condensation ()  cloud formation ()  precipitation () discharge ().  Evaporation: When water on the surface transforms () into water vapor.
 Transpiration: Evaporation which happens through plants.

 Precipitation: Water, snow, or the like which have formed clouds-- rain (), snow ()--and showers ().

 Snow accumulation: In cold regions (), snow doesn't melt. 
 Accumulation: Snow accumulates, and when permanent snow () compacts (), glaciers are formed.  
 Percolation: The phenomenon of water seaking into the ground.

Types of Clouds

The greatest contributor to the formation () of clouds is the existence of updrafts (). When air masses () contains water vapor carried up higher into the atmosphere () expand () due to drop () in air pressure () and then adiabatically cool (), the result is that humidity () rises () as the amount () of water vapor doesn't change. 


Though the dew point () will change according to air temperature () and the amount of water vapor prior to being lifted upward, when the dew point is exceeded, that results in supersaturated vapor (). When normal air containing particulates () reaches a supersaturation degree () of 1%, the condensation process () begins in which water vapor continues to coalesce into cloud condensation nuclei (), which in turn produce cloud droplets (), whether they be formed with water vapor that liquifies () or freezes ().
